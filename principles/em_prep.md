# What EM Does

I think of an Engineering Manager’s role in three core areas:

1. **Delivery (Business Impact)**
   My primary responsibility is ensuring the team delivers measurable outcomes, not just output. That means translating company goals into clear team OKRs and making the right tradeoffs on scope, speed, and quality.

2. **People (Raising the Bar)**
   I focus on building a high-performing team — hiring strong engineers, developing them toward the next level, and addressing performance gaps quickly. My goal is to create an environment where engineers have both clarity and autonomy.

3. **Alignment (Cross-functional Leadership)**
   A big part of the role is aligning engineering, product, design, and partner teams. I spend a lot of time driving clarity on priorities, resolving conflicts, and ensuring everyone is working toward the same outcomes.

Ultimately, I measure my success by:
- whether the team consistently delivers impact, and
- whether I’m leaving the team stronger than I found it.

# Low Performer

One situation I handled recently involved a staff-level engineer who was underperforming over two quarters.

**Context:**
He was delivering quickly, but the quality was inconsistent, which led to production issues. Over time, it became clear the root cause was a lack of engagement and ownership in the projects he was working on.

**Actions:**
I approached this in three steps:

1. **Clarify expectations early**
   I aligned with him on what “staff-level impact” looks like — not just speed, but quality, ownership, and influence across the team.

2. **Provide targeted support**
   - Reassigned him to a project with broader scope to re-engage him
   - Increased 1:1 coaching focused on quality and decision-making
   - Gave very specific, actionable feedback tied to real examples

3. **Create accountability**
   When improvement wasn’t consistent, I documented the gaps and moved toward a formal performance plan, partnering with HR to ensure fairness and clarity.

**Outcome:**
Despite support, the performance didn’t improve to expectations, and we proceeded with a formal PIP process.

**Reflection:**
If I could improve one thing, I would have acted faster. I waited to confirm the pattern, but at this level, early and direct intervention is more effective — both for the individual and the team.

# Roadmap Planning

My roadmap planning process starts from business goals and works top-down to execution.

**1. Start from company and org goals**
For example, if the goal is to improve conversion, I work with product and data to break that into measurable levers — such as discovery, ranking quality, or user engagement.

**2. Identify opportunities**
We combine three inputs:
- Data insights (funnel drop-offs, experiment results)
- Customer signals (feedback, behavior)
- Competitive benchmarking

From there, we generate a set of opportunities and estimate impact vs. effort.

**3. Prioritize with clear tradeoffs**
We prioritize based on expected impact, confidence, and cost. Typically:
- ~70–80% on high-confidence, iterative improvements
- ~20–30% on higher-risk “big bets” to unlock new surfaces

**4. Collaborate with the team on execution strategy**
I involve tech leads early to define:
- technical approach
- milestones
- risks and dependencies

**5. Translate into an executable roadmap**
We break initiatives into quarterly milestones, assign ownership, and define success metrics upfront.

**6. Iterate based on results**
We continuously adjust based on experiment outcomes and new data.

The key principle is: roadmap is not a list of features — it’s a plan to drive measurable business outcomes.

# Product Conflict

A recent example was a disagreement with product on launching a new visual search experience.

**Context:**
The product team was hesitant to launch because CTR dropped in early experiments. However, when we looked deeper, downstream metrics like add-to-cart and conversion were neutral.

**Challenge:**
The disagreement was about which metric to optimize — short-term engagement (CTR) vs. overall user value.

**Actions:**

1. **Align on goals**
   I reframed the discussion around our shared objective: improving end-to-end conversion and user experience, not just top-of-funnel metrics.

2. **Bring data into the conversation**
   I walked through the full funnel:
   - CTR decreased slightly
   - But downstream metrics remained stable
   - User effort decreased (fewer steps to find items)

3. **Propose a balanced approach**
   Instead of a full rollout, I suggested:
   - Gradual ramp
   - Continued monitoring of downstream metrics
   - Iterating on UX to recover CTR

**Outcome:**
We aligned on shipping with a controlled rollout and continued optimization.

**Key takeaway:**
In cross-functional conflicts, I focus on:
- aligning on shared goals,
- grounding decisions in data, and
- finding a path that balances speed with risk.

# Why DoorDash

What excites me about DoorDash is the combination of product complexity and real-world impact.

First, it’s a highly operational product — every decision affects a three-sided marketplace: customers, merchants, and dashers. That creates interesting engineering and product challenges around reliability, latency, and optimization.

Second, the product requires strong iteration and experimentation. Small improvements in discovery, ranking, or checkout can have a direct impact on conversion and retention, which aligns closely with the work I’ve been doing in search and discovery at Coupang.

Third, I’m particularly interested in the logistics and real-time aspects — building systems that need to make fast, accurate decisions under constraints is a space I want to go deeper in.

Overall, DoorDash sits at the intersection of product, data, and real-world execution — and that’s exactly where I’ve done my best work and want to continue growing.
